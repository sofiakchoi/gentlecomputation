can websites be loving?

Sometimes I lie next to my computer and listen to the fan spin out of control and the ambient heat radiating off its core feels like a body next to me.

In Jorge Luis Borges short story The Library of Babel, humanity exists in an expansive library composed of hexagonal shelves containing every possible combination of 25 characters. Every piece of knowledge, every fact about the future or past, is contained within the shelves. Librarians browse the galleries, searching for lines of meaning amongst nonsense. We live in a noisy world. The internet is as endless as Borges library. Chattering away, it assaults us with a barrage of constant information. Every second something is clicked, reposted, shared.
Fundementally, users do not understand the conduits through which they explore the web. There is a great mystification of technology, and a mythologizing of those who build and understand it. Tech billionaires demand to be viewed as a priestly caste, the holy channel our Machine-God travels through. This widening knowledge gap disempowers users, encouraging apathy and deference in those who do not believe they could build a better alternative. We have trended towards efficiency at the cost of humanity. In the platformatized world, everything must be smooth, optimized, and user-tested. The proliferation of LLMs is the natural conclusion to this efficiency economy, it offers a sedated, stupified life: never make a difficult choice, never search introspectively for an answer, ask your machine-god, and those who program it, to decide for you. Uber instead of walking, have ChatGPT write your emails, DoorDash all your groceries—forget all the inconvenience of your life, the essential human friction that begets discovery. The drive for technology based on convenience and efficiency is useful when we operate from a point of scarcity, but here, in the Global North, we are far past this point.
There once existed a sensitive web. A web made of individuals, deliberately choosing to form digital communities, not ones pushed together or artificially formed by algorithms. Their constructed digital spaces were messy, intimate, and exciting. Now, as we grow heavy with digital fatigue, many creative technologists are suggesting we look back to move forward.

✕ SPECULATIVE WEBS

What is the Internet to Artists?

Through the dominance of sleek, inoffensive, corporatized UI and templated website generators, we've boxed ourselves into a very narrow view of what a website can and should be. Artists are uniquely positioned to expand and experiment with these boundaries and definitions. Learning a bit of code and breaking out of these formats can encourage us to consider the potentialities of the website as a medium. Websites are rich with exploratory factors—materiality, usability, time.

✕ SPECULATIVE WEBS

The Website as a Medium

A blank HTML file has as much potential as a blank piece of paper. It is up to you, the creator, to determine what it can or cannot be. The capabilities of programming allow websites to be an interactive medium, how will you interact with/confront/love your users? How will you expand the concept of a website?

holy foundational texts of creative technology

Web Design as Architecture

Created by designer, writer, and programmer Malte Mueller, this website functions almost as a manifesto for how web design functions as a practice. I really enjoy any website-as-a-place metaphor, and I think this website demonstrates why they are so effective. The process of building a website is the process of crafting a digital space, leaving the user queues about how to conduct themselves and their interactions. It brings to mind geocities—websites literally organized into neighborhoods. Looking back on those websites is like wandering into an abandoned building, with traces of decay and broken links. A website can transport you to another time. Next time you visit a website, consider how its built environment makes you feel, the ways it challenges you or shows leniency, the cultures its nods to.

Dreamful Computing

Be free of your worries, pass them on to the dreamful media. Now go take back your life!

By scholars Ned Rossiter and Geert Lovink, Dreamful Computing will resonate with any young person disillusioned with technology. In psychoanalytic theory, dreams are the expression of our unconcious mind, representing our anxieties and repressed desires. Rossiter and Lovink argue that the omnipresence of social media and information capitalism has overloaded our brains with dopamine and depleted our unconcious, stifling our ability to dream. Their proposed theory of Dreamful Computing challenges our current Capitalist, move-fast-break-things methods of computation. Dreamful computing is uninterested in accountability, optimization, and efficiency. It is concerned with humanity, our salvation through dreams.

Handmade Webs

I evoke the term 'handmade web' to refer to web pages coded by hand rather than by software; web pages made and maintained by individuals rather than by businesses or corporations; web pages which are provisional, temporary, or one-of-a-kind; web pages which challenge conventions of reading, writing, design, ownership, privacy, security, or identity.

Written by digital media artist J.R. Carpenter, this essay discusses the art of handcoding simple webpages. The web was once a consalidation of disparate, handcoded sites. These sites were slow, messy, unoptimized—they reflected something personal about the soul of their creators. This intimate era of online interaction was replaced with our overwhelmingly corporate internet, dominated by platforms interested in obscuring the humanity of their users. Carpenter suggests a return to the DIY as a small act of resistance, a method of liberating ourselves from these online monopolies.

Poetic Websites

The Poetic Web is an ongoing exploration by artist and designer Chia Amisola. It is interested in a "more handmade, expressive, and intentional internet. It views site-making as a political, personal, and poetic act". She has an are.na channel full of interesting blocks related to the subject.
Beyond Amisola, the poetic web, and poetic websites, is a notion referenced by many new media artists, often in the same breath as the handmade web. The HTML Review, mentioned at the end of the page, is a journal of literature made for the web. They often feature pieces of poetry made digitally interactive by their creators (like this stunning one). Much in the way early modernists movements, like the Futurists, revolutionized the structure of poetry, websites provides artists with a myraid of new ways to examine the relationship between poem and reader.

There is merit in building something slowly, line-by-line. You can meditate in the cyclical process of writing, reloading, deleting and writing again. Part of the beauty of the web as a medium is its interaction with time and decay, a website will continue to grow for as long as you tend to it. Handcoding websites forces us to think about the labor of the internet in ways platformatization obfuscates. This labor demands intentionality, which users can feel when interacting with a handcoded site. Each choice, the construction of the site itself, was deliberate. Intimacy lies here, in the knowledge that the creator crafted this site with the user in mind: I made this for you.
What has been hidden from us in the smoothing over of our lives is that technology exists to connect us with each other. This is the true allure of the web, the links we make with other users, crossing the geographical boundaries between us. Self-commodification and algorithms that dictate interactions make organic connections difficult. The sleekness of social media platforms encourages the veneer of perfection from its users, the presentation of a carefully curated, airbrushed, "authentic" self. A human-centered web encourages imperfection. It is only through revealing these imperfections that we can truly know each other.
Before it was in our homes, the internet existed, here, beneath our feet. It's elemental properties are as natural as we are. You are just as capable of harnessing these elements as any software developer. The potential for a sensitive web lies within all of us. Our lives are not data, we cannot be optimized. I encourage you to embrace difficulty and inconvience, I encourage you to sit with discomfort. Understand these practices as part of your nature, commit to bettering yourself in a world that insists you stagnate. If we want a more human internet, we must understand our humanity first. Liberate yourself, create something small.


✕ ONLY THROUGH OUR TENDERNESS
✕ CAN WE MAKE THE INTERNET LOVING

Our technological liberation will only come through embracing the work of living. I dream of a patient internet, one that reaches through our screens and finds tender, imperfect humanity. Built by us, and for us, together.
